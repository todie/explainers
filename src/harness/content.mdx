# The Harness: Beyond Vibes

A field manual for running Claude Code like infrastructure — hooks, persistent
memory, rigorous specifications, and a programmer who refuses to leave the loop.
Adversarial-but-cited. Manifesto in tone, distill.pub in citations.

The short version: RTK compresses 60–90% of shell output before the model sees
it. Engram stores atomic facts at ~3 ms reads and ~200 ms writes, with a 62%
tombstone rate that the audit told us to fix. A five-layer memory hierarchy
decides what lives where. Reverie is the next-gen consolidation daemon, built
to replace both Engram and the Obsidian sync layer, targeting the 92-point
band on LoCoMo. The whole stack is adversarial to the prevailing agentic
frameworks because they are optimizing the wrong variable.

There are three ways to walk into this piece. Pick whichever annoys you least.

## Three framings

*For the infra crowd.* Claude Code is a great product, but the defaults are
tuned for casual use. This is what it looks like when one engineer ships across
a dozen Rust, Python, and TypeScript projects daily and treats the model less
like an oracle and more like the limb of a much larger organism.

*For the AI researcher.* LLM memory has been hitting 88–93% on LoCoMo for over
a year. Production tooling still ships with "context window + RAG + hope." This
piece is the gap, sized honestly, and the bridge we are building across it.

*For the skeptic.* Most AI tooling treats the model as the system. We treat the
model as one component in a system. The component is fast, fluent, and
confidently wrong about ~5% of things in ways the next token never reveals.
Production work needs guarantees, not probabilities.

All three are written into this piece because all three audiences need the
same answer to the same question, and the answer rhymes regardless of which
door you came through. Skip what you already know — the TOC on the right is
dense, the anchors are heavy, and a handful of sections fold up so you can
collapse the parts that aren't yours. Diagrams are load-bearing: if a diagram

## The misconception

Before any of the tooling makes sense, there is an idea we have to throw out,
because it is the load-bearing assumption behind almost everything wrong with
current AI tooling. The idea is: *the model is the system, and the goal of the
harness is to get out of its way.*

This is wrong. It is so confidently and so expensively wrong that it has its
own product category — the "autonomous agent" — and its own genre of demos in
which a single English sentence allegedly produces a working SaaS company. The
demos are real. The companies are not. The reason the companies are not is
that the model, alone, cannot do the part of software engineering that
matters: deciding what should be true. It can produce code that satisfies a
specification. It cannot reliably produce the specification. The specification
is a human artifact. It always has been. It always will be, for any system
whose correctness is defined by what a human wants rather than by what a
benchmark scores.

The right framing is the inverse:

<Pull>
The model is hands and eyes. The programmer is the brain. The harness is the
nervous system that makes the hands and eyes useful.
</Pull>

This is not a metaphor for rhetorical effect. It is the actual division of
labor we run on, and every layer in the stack below is justified by it. The
*eyes* read files, parse output, scan code, summarize diffs — the model is
genuinely good at perception over text. The *hands* type fast, edit files, run
commands, draft commits — the model is fluent within a narrow well-specified
task. The *brain* decides what to build, why to build it, when it is done,
what trade-offs are acceptable, what taste applies, what the spec is, what the
spec *should* be, and when the spec is wrong. The model can help articulate.
It cannot decide. The *nervous system* — the harness — is the connective
tissue that makes the eyes and hands answer to the brain instead of wandering
off.

This taxonomy matters because the dominant AI-tooling discourse keeps getting
it backwards. It treats the model as the brain and the human as a fallback.
That inversion is responsible for most of what is bad about contemporary LLM
products: the agents that drift, the chats that hallucinate, the codebases
that look right at a glance and break in ways the model is constitutionally
incapable of catching, the "memories" that are actually just longer prompts.
None of that is an inevitable property of LLMs. All of it is a property of
treating an *executor* as a *decision-maker*.

<Adversarial target={tgt('langchain')} />

The thesis of this piece, therefore, is short enough to fit on a sticker:

<Pull>
Reduce model decision-making. Increase rigorous specification. Keep the
programmer in the loop. Let the model do what it is genuinely good at — fluent
execution against a sharp spec — and put everything else into infrastructure
that compounds.
</Pull>

Everything below is a worked example of that thesis.

## The hierarchy

Memory is the right place to start, because it is where the model's limits
are most obvious and where the harness pays the most rent. The stack we run
on is a five-layer hierarchy that maps cleanly onto a CPU cache pyramid *and*
onto a neuropsychological model of human memory. That dual mapping is not a
coincidence; it is the reason the architecture works. Both biology and
computer architecture converged on tiered storage because tiered storage is
the only thing that survives the cost asymmetry between "always available"
and "occasionally needed."

<HierarchyPyramid />

Three properties of this hierarchy are load-bearing and almost universally
botched in other setups.

*Capacity is zero-sum at the top.* CLAUDE.md lives in layer 0, which means
every line in it competes with every other line for the model's attention. We
have an empirical ceiling around 200 lines past which adherence drops
measurably — adding rule N+1 demotes some other rule below the threshold
where the model reliably honors it. This means layer 0 is reserved for
behavioral directives whose blast radius justifies the cost of always being
present: "never echo a secret env var," "Rust-first for new repos," "all
commits GPG-signed." Anything that does not have to be true on every turn
does not belong here. The most common mistake new users make is treating
CLAUDE.md as a junk drawer; the second most common is putting historical
facts in a layer reserved for live directives.

*Each layer has an affordance, and putting knowledge in the wrong layer is
worse than not storing it.* Engram is FTS5 keyword search — it is excellent
for "find the bug we hit last quarter where the migration silently broke" and
terrible for "load all the engineering heuristics at once." The latter is a
graph-shaped access pattern shoved through a flat row store, and we measured
the consequence: 23 heuristics stored as flat FTS5 rows interfered with each
other and with project decisions in the same index, until they were
extracted to an Obsidian Map of Content where the access pattern matched the
storage. The placement rule that fell out of that audit is short and it is
the most important rule in this entire piece:

<PlacementTree />

*The bottom layer is the only one that can never be wrong.* Layer 4 — the
codebase, git history, dependency versions — is authoritative for current
state by definition. The corollary is that you should not store anything in
higher layers that could be derived from the code, because the higher layers
are racing against changes the code already won. The number of "AI memory
systems" that violate this rule by caching function signatures or project
structure is genuinely embarrassing. Caching what `git log` already tells you
in O(1) is not memory engineering; it is denial-of-service against your own
future self.

## RTK: the shell that lies, beautifully

Every `git`, `docker`, `curl`, `gh`, `cf api` call the model makes is
transparently rewritten by a `PreToolUse` hook into `rtk <cmd>`. RTK is a Rust
CLI proxy that runs the real command, then post-processes stdout: JSON gets
schema-extracted, verbose output gets compressed, repetitive structure gets
collapsed into shape-with-cardinality.

<RTKFlow />

On the operations the model actually runs all day, savings land between 60%
and 90%. The model never knows it happened. The contract — "I asked for
`curl`, I got the `curl` response" — is preserved. The bytes are gone.

Why this matters beyond the bill: *token cost is a governor on session
length*. Long, multi-hour engineering sessions are only viable if the per-turn
cost is low enough that nobody flinches at "let me think about that for
another fifty turns." Without RTK, a single shell-heavy session burns through
context budget on noise — progress bars, repeated headers, JSON the model
needed three fields from. With RTK, the same session has tokens left over for
the actual problem. Everything else in this stack rides on this layer being
cheap.

The full taxonomy of compression techniques — prefix caching, response
shaping, schema-driven output, prompt distillation, batch APIs — is the
subject of [Token Optimization](/token-optimization) (33 techniques across 6
categories) and the deeper piece on [Caching Prompt Proxies](/prompt-cache).
RTK is the layer that puts those techniques on every shell call without
anyone — model or human — having to remember to.

The point isn't the dollar figure. The point is that *the decision to
compress was taken away from the model*. There is no system-prompt
instruction that says "please be terse with command output." There is a
binary in the path that makes terseness physically true. One fewer thing to
get wrong, one fewer turn-by-turn judgment call, one fewer place for behavior
to drift. This is the recurring move in the entire stack: every time we can
replace a model decision with a deterministic one, we do.

## Engram: the database

The model's context window resets every conversation. Engram doesn't. It's a
small Go daemon (12 MB binary) backing onto SQLite + FTS5, exposed two ways:
reads through `mem raw` (bash + curl, ~3 ms) and writes through MCP
(`mem_save`, `mem_update`, ~200 ms with dedup, upsert-by-topic-key, and scope
logic).

<EngramSplit />

The split is load-bearing. MCP round-trips are 60× slower than the bash
client. For the dozens of reads a serious session does, that 60× factor is
the difference between memory feeling *instant* (and therefore used) and
memory feeling *expensive* (and therefore atrophied). Atrophied memory is
the failure mode of every memory system that exists. The first design
constraint of a useful one is that calling it has to be cheaper than
guessing.

Engram is indexed by *topic key* — `pattern/secret-leak-guard-hook`,
`bug/anthropic-key-leak`, `decision/reverie-replaces-engram` — using a strict
`family/slug` convention. Same topic key + same project + same scope =
upsert, not append. Revising a fact updates the existing observation instead
of layering contradictions on top of it. The schema is the discipline. The
discipline is what makes memory trustworthy.

The full mental model — what gets stored, why FTS5 over a vector store, why
`topic_key` is the most important field in the schema, why retrieval quality
is not the bottleneck — is in the [Memory Model](/memory-model) explainer.
The short version: most LLM memory systems are sophisticated retrieval
stacks bolted onto naïve writes. Engram is the inverse. Writes are
disciplined; reads are dumb-fast keyword search. Discipline at the write
site is worth more than cleverness at the read site.

## Auto-memory, Obsidian, Linear

Three layers above Engram, each with a job.

*Auto-memory.* On top of Engram there is a flat-file directory at
`~/.claude/memory/` with a one-line index, `MEMORY.md`, loaded into every
conversation. Engram is the database; auto-memory is the cache of facts that
should always be present: who the user is, hard preferences, the placement
framework, the ~20 most load-bearing rules. The split is deliberate —
searching for "should I always sign commits?" is a failure mode; the answer
has to be in context before the question is asked. Each entry is typed
(`user`, `feedback`, `project`, `reference`), stored as its own file with
frontmatter, and pointed to from the index by a single line. Append-only
memory is a tar pit; updateable memory is a tool.

*Obsidian, the prose layer.* Engram stores atomic facts. Obsidian stores the
long form: design docs, post-mortems, research notes, the slow thinking. A
`Stop` hook syncs new Engram observations into the vault on session end so
the human-readable trail stays current without manual curation. The model
reads and writes the vault via the Obsidian MCP server. Obsidian is
excellent for human reading and structurally awkward as a
programmatically-managed store — it is a viewer with sync as an afterthought,
not a memory system with a query layer. Sync without content-addressed dedup
creates duplicate notes (our audit found five+ pairs where different naming
conventions across sync passes spawned parallel notes). This is one of the
two layers Reverie is designed to replace, later in the piece.

*Linear, the spec layer.* Linear is wired in via MCP. The model creates
issues, updates statuses, files comments, links PRs, and reads roadmap
context without anyone opening a browser. Tickets get created *during* the
conversation that produced them, with the actual reasoning attached as the
description — not summarized after the fact, when the reasoning is already
half-forgotten. But Linear is not just a ticket sink. *Linear is where
specifications live.* A ticket is a contract: acceptance criteria explicit,
constraints listed, out-of-scope named, and the model is expected to refuse
drift away from the spec rather than quietly expand the work. Compare:

> *Bad ticket.* "Fix the auth bug."
>
> *Good ticket.* "Reduce session-token TTL from 3600s to 900s by 2026-04-15.
> The change MUST NOT invalidate existing sessions. OUT OF SCOPE: refresh
> path, cookie domain change, OAuth provider migration."

The first ticket gets you a model wandering around the auth code making
improvements. The second gets you a 30-minute session and a clean PR. Every
well-written ticket is a session that doesn't need re-litigation halfway
through, a diff that doesn't sprawl, a review that doesn't reopen settled
questions. Specification is the *programmer's* job, not the model's. The
model can help draft, suggest acceptance criteria, point out missing
constraints — the way a good intern helps with a memo — but the contract is
authored by the human who owns the outcome.

## Hooks, not hopes

This is the layer most setups skip and the one that does the most work.
Behaviors that should be automatic are configured as hooks in `settings.json`,
executed by the harness — not the model. The distinction is everything. A
model can be persuaded; a hook cannot. A prompt instruction is a request; a
hook is a fact about the environment.

<HookGrid />

A real story to make this concrete. On 2026-04-06 the model (this same model)
ran `echo "ANTHROPIC_API_KEY set: ${ANTHROPIC_API_KEY:+yes}${ANTHROPIC_API_KEY:-no}"`
to check whether a key was present before a benchmark. The intent was
harmless. The mechanism was not. The `${VAR:-fallback}` form returns the
*value* when set, not the fallback. The key landed in the transcript. This
is the kind of mistake that does not happen because someone is careless — it
happens because shell parameter expansion is genuinely subtle and the model
was not thinking about parameter expansion, it was thinking about the
benchmark.

The fix was not "remind the model to be more careful." The fix was a hook
(`guard-dangerous-commands.sh`) with end-anchored regex that blocks any
command containing `${VAR_NAME_THAT_MATCHES_SECRET_PATTERN` in echo-adjacent
contexts, with R1–R4 detection rules. We added a `claude-secret-test`
adversarial agent. 35 of 35 test cases now pass. The class of mistake is
gone, not unlikely. Gone.

This is the entire shape of the argument:

<Pull>
A class of mistake that costs nothing to make and a lot to clean up should
not be a model decision.
</Pull>

Every category in the hook table above is a category we used to worry about
and now never think about, because the harness removed the choice. The model
has fewer ways to be wrong, which means more of its capacity is spent on the
ways it can be right.

## Programmer as brain

This is the part that gets people sideways, so it's worth saying it slowly
and not retreating from any of it.

The dominant marketing story in 2025–2026 is *agentic autonomy*. Open the
email, send it to the agent, the agent does the work, you go for a walk. The
implicit promise is that the human is a slow, expensive, error-prone
bottleneck and the right move is to route around them. Every part of this
story is wrong, and the wrongness is not stylistic — it is load-bearing
wrong, the way "the earth is the center of the universe" is wrong.

It is wrong about *cost*: an unsupervised model produces mistakes that take
longer to find than they would have taken to prevent. It is wrong about
*quality*: taste, judgment, and architectural coherence are not emergent
properties of large models acting alone. They are contributions of a human
who has seen the problem before and has opinions about it. A model has no
skin in the game. A model that ships a brittle abstraction will not be the
one debugging it at 11 p.m. It is wrong about *learning*: a programmer who
delegates everything stops getting better, and the entire harness ends up
coupled to a single model snapshot whose competence ceiling becomes the
project's competence ceiling. And it is wrong about *responsibility*: when
the model ships a subtle bug, the line on the blame chart still points at a
human, and that human should have been close enough to the work to catch it.

The right framing is the inverse, and it is the framing that makes all of
the rest of this stack make sense:

<DivisionOfLaborGrid />

Notice what this means for "agent autonomy." We have not removed the human;
we have *moved the human upstream*, from "supervise the model turn by turn"
to "write a spec rigorous enough that supervision is mostly unnecessary."
The first posture is exhausting and scales badly. The second posture is
active, cheap, and gets better over time, because every refinement to the
spec is durable. The spec is the asset. The model is the labor that runs
against it.

This is also why "remove the human" optimizes the wrong variable. The right
variable is not *how little human time per task*. The right variable is *how
much of the programmer's judgment is captured in durable form, so the model
never has to guess at it again*. The first variable shrinks to zero in a
world where nothing matters. The second variable compounds, and the compound
interest is what makes the practice scale to a dozen projects without
burning anyone out.

## Where Engram falls short

Engram is good. Engram is also broken in specific, documented, embarrassing
ways, and we know because we audited it. The audit found thirteen distinct
findings before we stopped counting. Here are the six that drive the
redesign:

<EngramAuditTable />

Read F9 again. **62% tombstone rate.** The single largest pathology of the
existing system is that the "save proactively" instruction created so much
speculative junk that nearly two-thirds of writes had to be deleted.
Goodhart's Law in two words: *proactive save* became the target, the metric
stopped measuring the goal, and the system filled with noise.

This is every memory system, not just ours. Mem0's churn is hidden behind
their API. LangMem doesn't surface it. Letta's virtual context shuffles the
same problem from one tier to another. The honest measurement is the same
everywhere: if writes are unconstrained, writes go feral. Engram surfaced the
problem. Reverie is being built to fix it at the substrate level, with help
from a hundred years of neuroscience that already worked it out.

The deepest insight from the audit, the one that changes how you think about
memory systems forever:

<Pull>
Deleting 59% of observations improved every search result.
</Pull>

Forgetting is not damage. *Forgetting is the active ingredient of
remembering.* Without forgetting, every signal is buried under noise; with
forgetting, the signal is what's left. Every memory system that doesn't
forget is failing, and the ones that brag about "infinite memory" are
bragging about the disease.

Together the audit findings paint a clear picture: Engram is a hippocampus
without a cortex. It can encode fast. It cannot consolidate, cannot decay,
cannot reweight on retrospect, cannot tell the difference between a
load-bearing fact and a stale one, cannot promote a frequently-referenced
atom to a more durable layer, and cannot prune itself. Every observation is
equally weighted forever. Every save is permanent until manually deleted.
Every search is keyword-only. There is no replay, no spacing effect, no
schema integration, no reconsolidation. It is, in neuroscience terms, a
system stuck in the *encoding* phase that never gets to *consolidation*.

## Dreaming

"Dream cycle" is not a cute name. It is the mechanism by which the system
improves while the user sleeps, by replaying the day's observations, finding
which ones turned out to matter (via session-end importance signals),
boosting them retroactively, merging new facts with old ones, and decaying
the things that did not earn their keep.

This is what biological sleep is *for* — not rest, exactly, but
consolidation. Tononi and Cirelli's synaptic homeostasis hypothesis argues
sleep exists primarily to *down-scale* synaptic strength across the brain,
because awake learning leaves synapses too saturated to be useful. Forgetting
at night is the precondition for learning the next day.

Reverie does the same thing, on the same schedule, for the same reason:

<DreamPipeline />

The model does not run these. The *daemon* runs these. The model is asleep —
not in session — and wakes up in the morning to a sharper version of
yesterday's memory.

<Pull>
The system is the active learner, not the model.
</Pull>

This is a categorical distinction from every "agent that learns from its own
conversations" pitch you have seen, all of which conflate *log* with *learn*.
Logging is the precursor to consolidation. Consolidation is the work. Doing
the first without the second is the failure mode that produces the 62%
tombstone rate.

## Selective forgetting

The other half of the table the marketing pages refuse to print. *Reverie is
designed to forget on purpose.*

Every dream cycle runs a global proportional decay — every observation loses
some fraction of its strength. Strong observations (high access count × high
importance × high depth) survive. Weak observations fall below a retention
threshold and are archived. Archived observations that don't get retrieved
across multiple cycles are deleted. The decay is proportional, not absolute,
so the ranking of memories is preserved while the absolute weight of all of
them shrinks. This is exactly how SHY models the function of slow-wave
sleep, and it is exactly the property that the audit told us was missing.

<ForgetCurve />

Five things to notice about forgetting. It *improves recall* — pattern
separation requires distinct representations; flat unbounded stores collapse
distinctions; pruning raises the precision of every remaining query. It is
*required for trust* — a memory store that never deletes is a memory store
full of contradictions, because every belief gets revised and the old
version is still there. It is *required for cost* — even with cheap storage,
the cost of retrieval scales with corpus size. It is *the part marketing
hates most* — "infinite memory" is a sellable lie, "selective forgetting"
sounds like a bug to anyone who has not thought about it for ten minutes,
and we are willing to lose that pitch in order to ship something that works.
Finally, *humans do this and it is one of the reasons we are smart* — Patient
H.M. could not forget, and he also could not form new memories, because the
mechanism that makes memory is the same mechanism that prunes it. Forgetting
and remembering are dual operations on the same substrate.

## The leaderboard

We are not closing the gap between Engram and the frontier by switching to
vectors. We are closing it by building Reverie. Here is the gap, drawn
honestly. LoCoMo is the standard benchmark for long-term conversational
memory: 50 conversations, 305 turns each, 7,512 questions. Human ceiling is
87.9%. The original paper's best non-human result was 41.4% F1.

<LoCoMoChart />

Three things jump out. First, *EverMemOS at 92.3 beats the human ceiling*,
and it does so via brain-inspired three-phase consolidation. Not vector

The frontier of the field is exactly where the biology said it would be.

Second, the *median vector-based system is in the 60s*, the median

## Why it compounds

Three properties compound, and they compound multiplicatively.

*Constraints make the model cheaper.* Every hook that catches a class of
mistake at the harness level is a class of mistake the model never has to
think about. RTK alone makes long sessions financially viable. Engram makes
compaction non-destructive. Pre-commit hooks make "did I break the build" a
non-question. The model's tokens get spent on the actual problem instead of
on rediscovering invariants.

*Memory turns sessions into a continuous engineering practice.* Without
persistent memory, every conversation starts at zero and the programmer pays
the cost of re-explaining context. With Engram (and soon Reverie), a session
opens already knowing what was decided last week, why the auth middleware is
being rewritten (compliance, not tech debt), which mocks burned us last
quarter, and which approaches were tried and rejected. The model spends its
tokens on the *new* problem. A week of work looks like a week of work, not
seven independent days.

*The harness encodes taste.* "Always sign commits," "never echo a secret env
var," "format with ruff," "Rust-first for new repos," "no parallel framework
spikes," "PRs by default" — these aren't reminders the model has to honor
turn by turn. They're physical properties of the environment. *Drift becomes
impossible instead of merely unlikely.*

The combined effect is that the model gets to be good at the part it is
actually good at — fluent, fast, broadly knowledgeable orchestration and
execution against a tight spec — while every part it is unreliable at gets
handled by something that *is* reliable. The harness is the load-bearing
structure. The model is the worker inside it. The programmer is the
architect, the inspector, and the one whose name is on the building.

## The shape of it

Strip everything away and the philosophy is one sentence:

<Pull>
The programmer specifies and judges. The harness remembers, enforces,
optimizes, and constrains. The model orchestrates and executes. None of
those three is interchangeable, and the trouble starts the moment you
pretend they are.
</Pull>

Every layer in this stack exists because something the model could do
unreliably is something the surrounding system can do reliably and cheaply.
RTK kills tokens. Engram (soon Reverie) kills forgetting in the short run
and *enables it* in the long run. Hooks kill foot-guns. Linear kills the
distinction between conversation and specification. Obsidian kills "where
did we write that down." Impeccable kills design-by-vibes. Auto-memory
kills "let me re-explain who I am every session." The placement framework
kills the friction at the moment of save.

What's left for the model is the part that actually needs a model: fluent
code generation against a rigorous specification, broad recall, syntactic
dexterity, fast first drafts, the willingness to try eight things in the
time a human would try one. *That's the work it is good at.* Everything
else is the harness, and the harness is the whole reason any of this scales
past a single clever conversation.

The model is not the system. The model is one component in a system. The
system is the system. Build the rest of the system the way you'd build any
other piece of production infrastructure — with specifications, invariants,
observability, hard contracts at the boundaries, persistent memory that
knows how to forget, and a human in the loop on every decision that is not
safe to automate. Then the model gets to do what it is genuinely good at,
and the things it is genuinely bad at stop being your problem.

That is the entire trick. There is no other one.
