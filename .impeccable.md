# Impeccable — explain.todie.io

Design context and principles for [todie/explainers](https://github.com/todie/explainers).
All design decisions — new explainers, components, typography, motion, copy — should
trace back to this document. If a decision doesn't map to one of these principles,
either the decision or the principle needs to change.

## Design Context

### Users

**Primary:** Technical peers — HN-crowd engineers, infra people, AI researchers,
Claude Code power users. They arrive with high context: they already know what an
LLM is, what a cache is, what an LSP does. They are not here to be sold anything;
they are here because a specific title or a social share promised a non-obvious
angle on something they care about.

**Context of use:** Long-form reading sessions, usually on desktop, usually after
clicking through from a share link (Threads, HN, Bluesky). Some scroll to the
bottom; most scan the diagrams and the section headers first and decide whether
to invest. The TOC exists so they can teleport.

**Job to be done:** "Give me the mental model I don't have yet — one that makes
the rest of my career cheaper." Every explainer should leave the reader with at
least one diagram they'll re-draw on a whiteboard later.

### Brand Personality

**Three words:** rigorous, calm, generous.

- **Rigorous** — claims are load-bearing. If something is cited, the citation
  is real. If something is a guess, it says so. No hand-waving past the hard part.
- **Calm** — the prose doesn't perform urgency. No "Let's dive in!", no
  exclamation points, no attention-grabbing animations. Confidence does not need
  volume.
- **Generous** — the author already did the hard reading so the reader doesn't
  have to. Links out. Shows the work. Gives away the good stuff in the first
  third of the page instead of hiding it behind a fold.

**Emotional goal:** the reader closes the tab feeling *smarter*, not entertained.
The feeling should be closer to "I just read a good paper" than "I just watched
a good video."

**Voice split:** `explain.todie.io` is the voice above.
`rant.todie.io` (planned, same content, different prose) is the loud sibling —
irreverent, opinionated, willing to be wrong out loud. Design tokens are shared;
voice and copy diverge.

### Aesthetic Direction

**References (aim here):**
- [ciechanowski.dev](https://ciechanowski.dev/) — Bartosz Ciechanowski. Interactive
  canvases, pristine typography, generous whitespace, no decoration that isn't
  load-bearing. The gold standard for "rigorous AND generous."
- [distill.pub](https://distill.pub/) — academic-adjacent, diagram-forward,
  measured voice, explicit equations, legible at dense zoom levels.

**Anti-references (explicitly avoid):**
- **Corporate SaaS marketing** — no gradient hero selling nothing, no
  "Trusted by" logo rails, no mascot illustrations, no CTA buttons, no
  newsletter popups.
- **Medium / dev.to blog posts** — no centered 680px column with clap buttons
  and social rails. No "5 min read" badges. No author card with follow buttons.
- **AI-generated slop aesthetic** — no generic pastel gradients, no emoji spam
  in copy, no "Let's dive in!" filler, no "In this article we will explore..."
  preambles, no bulleted lists that are actually just sentences.
- **Academic paper PDFs / arxiv-vanity austerity** — distill.pub's
  diagram-forward clarity is a positive reference, but the site should NOT
  feel like a dry monograph. Personality, color, and confident opinion are
  welcome. We're not publishing; we're teaching peers.

**Theme:** dark-only for now. Background is near-black (`#030712`), surfaces are
cool-gray (`#111827`/`#1a2332`), borders are quiet (`#1f2937`/`#374151`).
A light mode is on the "Maybe" list in ROADMAP.md and is not a priority.

**Liberal use of visuals, charts, and diagrams.** Prose explains; visuals
demonstrate. An explainer without at least three original figures is missing
its main teaching surface — and good explainers typically have more. Every
non-trivial claim that involves structure, comparison, timing, magnitude,
or spatial relation should be accompanied by a figure, not as decoration
but as the load-bearing evidence for the claim. Concretely:

- **Prefer SVG over PNG.** SVG scales, theme-responds, and can be animated
  cheaply. Reserve raster formats for photographs or pre-rendered previews.
- **Chart the data you cite.** If a paper reports percentages, plot them.
  If a study counted something, show the count. If a timeline spans
  decades, draw the timeline. Don't leave numbers stranded in prose when
  a figure would communicate them in a glance.
- **Build original figures, don't import stock art.** Every figure is
  hand-assembled for *this* explainer's argument. No clipart, no
  icon-libraries-as-illustration, no generic diagrams copied from
  elsewhere. If the argument is yours, the figure is yours.
- **Caption every figure.** Use `<figcaption>` with "Figure N." and a
  one-line description of the point. Captions are often read more than
  body text — they are where the figure's claim gets made explicit.
- **Figures work at two scales.** A reader scrolling past grasps the
  figure in one second from shape alone. A reader stopping can zoom in
  and read axis labels, legend entries, and annotations.
- **Interactivity only when it earns its keep.** Auto-playing demos
  and hover tooltips are fine. Click-to-expand accordions are not
  interactivity — they are hiding, and they violate the "clean and
  sharp, not gamified" rule.
- **Rule of thumb:** if you've written 400+ words without a figure, you
  owe the reader a figure.

**Clean and sharp, not gamified.** This is the single most important aesthetic
rule — more important than any individual color or font choice. The site is
an essay collection, not a dashboard. Concretely:

- **No click-to-expand accordions in the body.** Readers came to read. Show
  the content. Hiding paragraphs behind `▾` chevrons is dashboard UX, not
  essay UX. If a section is long, use a heading. If something is truly
  optional, use a native `<details>` element with no custom chrome.
- **Hairline rules, not rounded cards.** Separate sections with
  `border-top: 1px solid #1f2937` and whitespace. Avoid `border-radius` on
  content containers — rounded boxes feel like app UI. Reserve `border-radius`
  for actual controls (buttons, badges), not for prose blocks.
- **Typography does the hierarchy, not color.** Use size, weight, italics,
  small-caps, and whitespace to signal importance. Coloring every subheading
  in the explainer accent is a gamification reflex — resist it. The accent
  earns the hero, the TOC progress bar, inline links, and ONE blockquote
  style. It does not earn section labels, card borders, or category pills.
- **No colored chips on every item.** A "PHYSICS" pill next to every example
  title turns a reading experience into a filter UI. Use a small-caps prefix
  or an inline italic tag instead, and only when the tag actually helps the
  reader scan.
- **No gradient-on-hover surfaces.** No backgrounds that shift tint when
  something is "active". Essays don't have active state.
- **Semantic color is allowed when it does work.** ProsCons can legitimately
  use green for advantages and red for failure modes because color encodes
  the binary. A mode-comparison table can use distinct colors per mode so
  the reader can track a column across rows. In those cases, restrain the
  color — put it in small-caps headers, not in card fills or full borders.
- **One source of color per page.** Either the explainer's accent (chrome,
  links, TOC, one blockquote style) OR a semantic palette that does teaching
  work — never both competing at the same zoom level. If a section uses a
  pros/cons green/red split, the chrome accent stays out of that section.

**Typography:**
- Body: Inter, 400/500/600/700/800.
- Mono: JetBrains Mono, 400/600/700. Used for metadata, code, and topology
  labels — not for body copy.
- Line height 1.6 body, 1.1 on hero headings. Gradient headings are allowed
  but used sparingly (hero + occasional section titles, never inline).

**Color strategy:** each explainer picks one accent from the shared palette
(`--blue #60a5fa`, `--purple #a855f7`, `--green #4ade80`, `--yellow #facc15`,
`--red #f87171`, `--cyan #22d3ee`) and commits to it. The accent colors the
header gradient, the TOC progress bar, the "current section" borderLeft, and
any decorative glows. No explainer gets two accents; restraint is the point.

**Iconography:** emoji rendered via the Twemoji v14.0.2 SVG CDN (see
[`src/shared/Emoji.jsx`](src/shared/Emoji.jsx) — ensures consistent color
rendering across WSL2/Linux/Windows where system emoji fonts can't be
trusted. SVG line icons are used only for functional affordances (the lock
glyph in the private-docs button); nothing else gets custom icon art.

**Motion:** subtle, slow, infinite loops that the reader ignores. `fadeUp` on
entry, `pulse` on ambient glows, `flowDown` on matrix-rain-style decorative
backgrounds. Nothing "performs" — motion exists to soften state changes and
hint at liveness, not to grab attention. Respect `prefers-reduced-motion`
whenever a new animation ships.

**Layout density:** wider than Medium (`maxWidth: 900` for content, not 680),
generous vertical rhythm (`gap: 80px` between sections), scroll-margin-top so
TOC jumps don't clip under the fixed nav. Content-first, chrome-light: the
fixed nav is 40px thin, the TOC floats, and nothing else competes with the
explainer itself.

### Design Principles

These are the rules. When a design decision is ambiguous, the principle higher
in this list wins.

1. **The idea does the work, not the chrome.** If a decoration, animation, or
   flourish isn't load-bearing — if the explainer reads just as well without it
   — cut it. Diagrams are load-bearing. Background glows usually aren't. Every
   pixel earns its spot.

2. **Respect the reader's time and context.** Readers arrive already smart;
   don't re-explain the basics, don't preface, don't "Let's dive in!". Put the
   non-obvious insight in the first third of the page. The TOC is a contract
   that any section can be entered cold.

3. **Restraint over richness.** One accent color per explainer. One mono font,
   one body font. One motion vocabulary. One emoji style (Twemoji). Variety is
   achieved by composition, not by adding new primitives. Every new color,
   font, or animation is a tax on future consistency.

4. **Dark, technical, confident — never corporate.** No gradient hero pitching
   nothing. No "Trusted by" rails. No mascot. No stock illustration. No clap
   buttons. No follow-me author cards. If a pattern feels at home on a SaaS
   marketing site, it does not belong here.

5. **Show the work.** Real citations, real code, real diagrams. If something is
   uncertain, say so. If something is guessed, label the guess. Generosity with
   source material is how we earn the reader's trust — and how we separate
   `explain.todie.io` from AI-generated summaries of the same topics.
